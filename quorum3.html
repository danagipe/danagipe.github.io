Unbearable: Can We Please Stop Encouraging This Puerile Thrillseeker?

By Sam-I-Am, Calistril 3, 317

In recent months there has arisen a sort of cultural fascination with (read: mass psychosis of) the idea of heroism. This societal regression is due in no small part to the surging popularity of the holovid cartoon “Halaina’s Heroes” and other bandwagoning copycats which seek to reimagine the heroic myths of Old Golarian with modern characters for a modern audience. One might defend the inherent naivete of such efforts by pointing out their obvious target audience: children. Indeed, I consider it a commendable and worthwhile endeavor to instill our youth with ideals of justice and self-sacrifice; but for mature audiences such lessons ought to be tempered with pragmatism. Alas, these stories, simplified for a child’s mind, seem to have made a cultural impact far beyond the generation they were intended to reach. Already we find ourselves practically drowning in amateur do-gooders with all the unchecked ego and self-assured moral righteousness that comes with the “hero” label; meanwhile the media trumpets excessive laudations upon them, the Plenara does nothing to rein them in, and it is we the taxpayers who are left to foot the bill when these “heroic” acts are inevitably bungled

Nowhere is there a more apt example of this exact buffoonery than in Absalom Station’s very own Paago the Skitterbear.

For the blessedly ignorant, here is a quick description of the aforementioned figure: imagine a ferocious, six-armed, 9-foot-tall bear piloted by the brain of an excitable skittermander. Now imagine that incongruous amalgamation gallivanting about a space station as if he were Argal the Redcape himself, using restricted military-grade tech to, as he so eloquently puts it, “help”. For that is indeed his one and only explicitly stated goal, to “help”.

Now imagine, dear reader, that you were to find yourself, Goddess forfend, accosted by some ne’er-do-well drifter in Fogtown. Naturally, you would in response transmit an emergency comm. In such a situation you would probably hope that a trained professional might be dispatched to your rescue, perhaps an AbSec unit. If instead you found yourself defended by a multilimbed mutant who might just as soon be persuaded to “help” the ruffian relieve you of your credstick, you would be justified in feeling that you had not received what you wanted and that perhaps someone should have told that bear to stay home and taken his emergency scanner away.

You might find this scenario to be far-fetched. After all, how could someone who has been called “a beacon of hope and positivity” by the oft-vaunted FTL News possibly be confused about who needed help in such a situation? To that I would say, dear reader, and to the editors of the sopping rag FTLN, who long ago abandoned journalist integrity in favor of donor-approved fluff, “Perhaps you ought to brush up on your Veskarium history.”

I am speaking of the Veskarium’s conquest of Vesk-3, the home planet of the skittermanders, which, according to the Veskarium’s remarkably intact pre-gap records, occurred in 208 PG. When vesk representatives descended from their orbital fleet to parlay with the native species, they were dumbfounded by the response they received from the skittermander leaders. For when the ruthless invaders demanded that the skittermanders submit to Veskarium rule, their compliant reply was thus: “Oh, you’re conquering us? How can we help?”

Let us not forget, dear reader, that the savage exterior of the bear, that noble extinct species of Old Golarion, is simply a shell, a costume that obfuscates Paago’s true identity. For beneath the fur and teeth and rippling muscles he is, after all, a skittermander, replete with the foibles and flaws of that species. Given this insight, is it really prudent that we should allow such a creature to respond to emergencies? Can we really trust the judgement of this alien mind, whose forebears (pun unintended) surrendered to authoritarian rule with nary a peep? I say no. I say that the solution to criminal activity in Absalom Station is not pinning our hopes on some ursine adrenaline junkie who will make for great PR moments but inevitably fail to curtail crime rates; rather we ought to support the existing AbSec, which was created for this express purpose and was performing the job admirably until the disastrous Melacruz administration slashed its operating budget by half.

In closing, I will offer my editorial services, free of charge, to FTLN, who may at their leisure print a retraction of their statement “[Paago is] a beacon of hope and positivity” with my own amendment: “Paago is an unhinged lunatic who presents an immediate danger to the good citizens of Absalom Station and represents a disastrous trend spreading throughout the Pact Worlds."
